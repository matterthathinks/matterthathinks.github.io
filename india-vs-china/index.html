Why China Succeeded

and what India can learn

GDP State Capacity Programmes Education Industry Infrastructure Missed Sources

India · China · Development Economics

In 1980, India's economy (PPP) was larger than China's. By 2024, China's GDP is 5× bigger. This is a comprehensive, data-driven attempt to understand why — and what India can still do about it.

01

China and India started from near-identical economic positions at independence. The gap that opened between them is not a story of luck — it is a story of choices, institutions, and the capacity to execute.

India's path

1947
Independence with colonial institutions
Inherited civil service, legal system, some railways. But also inherited deep underdevelopment, low literacy, and weak state manufacturing capacity.
1950s–70s
Nehruvian socialism — heavy industry focus
PSUs, IITs, steel plants. "Commanding heights" model. License Raj constrains private enterprise. Manufacturing stagnates despite intent.
1991
Liberalisation — 13 years after China
Balance-of-payments crisis forces opening. IT services boom emerges — unplanned, but leveraging India's English and IIT advantages. Services replace manufacturing as growth engine.
2000s–present
Services-led, job-light growth
Strong GDP growth, but manufacturing share stagnant. Millions enter working age with insufficient formal employment. Informality stays above 80%.

China's path

1949
Revolution — single-party state capacity
Mao's literacy campaigns begin immediately. Local party officials' careers tied to literacy metrics. State can coerce compliance at scale. India lacked this mechanism entirely.
1966–76
Cultural Revolution — a genuine setback
Universities closed. Intellectuals persecuted. A devastating 10-year interruption to education and research. Yet the mass literacy base built in the 1950s survived.
1978
Deng Xiaoping — Four Modernizations
Science, technology, industry, and defence as four pillars. Gaokao restored. Economy opens. Crucially: a literate, increasingly educated workforce is already waiting.
1980s–2000s
Export manufacturing boom
SEZs, factory infrastructure, global supply chain integration. 800 million lifted out of poverty. Manufacturing share of GDP surges to 30%+.
02

The Foundational Argument:
State Capacity

Most comparisons focus on what India and China chose to do differently. The deeper question is what each country was capable of doing. State capacity — the ability to design, fund, and enforce policy at scale — is the precondition that made China's programmes possible. It is where the real divergence begins.

"India in 1947 lacked the state capacity to run a mass literacy campaign. China had a single-party apparatus willing to coerce compliance. The real comparison is not just policy preference — it is state capacity as a precondition for policy execution."
China's mechanism
Political incentives, not ideology
What drove China's mass education was not communist ideology alone — it was fiscal decentralization combined with political accountability. Under Mao, local party officials' careers literally depended on literacy metrics. Under Deng, the 1986 Compulsory Education Law made local governments financially responsible for 9-year schooling. The incentive structure aligned bureaucratic ambition with education outcomes.
India's constraint
Democracy and state fragmentation
India's federal, democratic structure — a genuine strength in many ways — made large-scale coercive mobilisation impossible. Centre-state tensions, political competition, and patronage networks meant education policy was inconsistently enforced. The Right to Education Act came in 2009 — 60 years after China's initial literacy drives. Near-universal enrollment was achieved only around 2000, where China reached it by the mid-1980s.
The lesson
Execution, not just design
India designed good policies — Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan, mid-day meal schemes, RTE. The failure was delivery. Teacher absenteeism, infrastructure gaps, and urban-rural quality divides meant enrollment didn't translate to learning. ASER reports consistently show that 50%+ of Class V students cannot read a Class II text. The problem is not intent — it is the pipeline from policy to classroom.
China's enforcement
The 1986 Compulsory Education Law
After Deng's 1978 reforms, China enacted the Compulsory Education Law in 1986, mandating 9 years of schooling for all children and giving local governments financial responsibility. This combined national mandate with local accountability. Central government could monitor, local officials were responsible, parents had legal standing. The mechanism worked.
India's trajectory
Late, uneven progress
India's primary enrollment rate in 1950 was ~43%. China's was similar — but China made universal basic education a state mobilization goal from 1949. By 1978, when China opened its economy, it had a literate, trainable workforce ready to staff factories. India was still catching up in basic literacy when its own economic opening arrived in 1991. The workforce wasn't ready the same way.
The nuance
Neither model is simply "better"
China's authoritarian capacity enabled rapid execution but also produced disasters (Great Leap Forward, Cultural Revolution). India's democracy is slower but more resilient and corrective. The question is whether India can find democratic mechanisms for high-execution governance — as South Korea and Taiwan eventually did within their own political trajectories.

The sharpest question: Was India's choice really a choice, or a constraint? India in 1947 lacked the state capacity to run a mass literacy campaign. China had a single-party apparatus. The real comparison is not just policy preference — it's state capacity as a precondition for policy execution. Critiquing India's choices without acknowledging this asymmetry is unfair. Ignoring it is also misleading.

03

Programmes, Policies
and Deliberate Bets

From the 1950s onward, China made a series of sequenced, compounding investments in science, technology, and industry. India made some good bets (IITs, ISRO, DAE) but missed the connective tissue between them — the programmes that link research to commercialisation, and education to employment.

China · 1956

12-Year Science & Technology Plan

The first strategic S&T roadmap

  • China's first national S&T plan identified 57 key tasks including atomic energy, electronics, and jet propulsion — 8 years before India had an equivalent framework.
  • Crucially, it framed scientific development as a state security and development priority, not merely an academic exercise.
  • India had the 1958 Science Policy Resolution (Nehru) but lacked comparable institutional follow-through and execution machinery.
China · March 1986

Programme 863 — State High-Tech R&D Plan

Seven fields, one letter, a transformed nation

  • Four scientists wrote to Deng Xiaoping warning that China was falling behind the global tech revolution. Deng approved the programme within 48 hours. Named for its date: March (3rd month) 1986.
  • Targeted 7 fields: biology, aerospace, information technology, lasers, automation, energy, new materials. Marine technology added 1996. Initial fund: 10 billion RMB — 5% of all government spending that year.
  • Inspired by Reagan's Strategic Defense Initiative. Over 20 years: 120,000+ research papers, 8,000+ patents, 1,800+ national industry standards.
  • India had no equivalent systematic high-tech R&D programme at this scale in this period.
China · August 1988

Programme TORCH — Industrialisation & Incubation

The programme that built China's startup infrastructure

  • Approved by State Council to bridge lab and market: created Science and Technology Industrial Parks (STIPs), Technology Business Incubators (TBIs), Innofund seed capital, and a Venture Guiding Fund.
  • The first incubator was built in Wuhan (1987), Shanghai followed (1988). The idea: artificially create clustering by concentrating resources, talent, and capital to a critical threshold.
  • Result: China now has 11,000+ incubators and accelerators. India has ~950 (2023). India's Startup India programme — the closest equivalent — came in 2016, 28 years later.
  • Israel, Singapore and China are cited as the only three countries to successfully create innovation clusters by deliberate government design.
China · 1995 / 1998

Project 211 & Project 985 — World-Class Universities

Building the top of the pyramid, deliberately

  • Project 211 (1995): Named for the 21st century and 100 institutions. Made research universities compete for major government grants, forcing them to build faculty, labs, and infrastructure. 112 universities ultimately included.
  • Project 985 (1998): Jiang Zemin declared China must have "world-class universities." Started with 9 (C9 League = China's Ivy League), expanded to 39. Billions poured into faculty, international hiring, and research infrastructure.
  • Then Double First Class (2015–17): 140 universities targeted to become world-class by 2050. China overtook the USA in Nature Index natural-sciences output in 2022.
  • Crucially: China did this while maintaining mass primary education. India built IITs but without the same simultaneous investment in the layer beneath them.
China · 2015

Made in China 2025

Industrial upgrading for the next century

  • Targeted 10 strategic sectors: AI, electric vehicles, semiconductors, biotechnology, aerospace, advanced materials, robotics, maritime engineering, rail equipment, agricultural machinery.
  • Explicit goal: move from "factory of the world" to "innovator for the world." Reduce dependence on foreign technology in critical sectors.
  • India's equivalent — National Manufacturing Policy (2011), PLI schemes (2020+) — is more recent and less integrated with a broader innovation ecosystem.
India · 1951–2016

India's IIT / IIM / AIIMS programme

Building the top — without the full pyramid

  • India's strategy was elite-first: IIT Kharagpur (1951), IIM Calcutta (1961), AIIMS Delhi (1956). By 2000: only 7 IITs, 6 IIMs, 1 AIIMS — a deliberately scarce tier.
  • These institutions created India's global tech reputation, IT sector, and diaspora presence. They were a good bet — not the problem.
  • The failure: India built islands of excellence without simultaneously building the vocational layer, the research-to-market pipeline, or the primary education quality beneath them.
  • Nehru called IIT Kharagpur "a symbol of India, a fine monument of what free India is building." The trickle-down theory of human capital — that elite engineers would lift all boats — was the implicit, ultimately incomplete, assumption.

What India's programme list is missing: A TORCH equivalent (incubators, STIPs, commercialisation pipeline). A Project 211 equivalent (systematic funding of 100+ universities). A vocational education strategy tied to industrial demand. These are not gaps in elite education — they are gaps in the ecosystem around elite education.

04

Education & Human Capital:
The Full Pyramid

Development economists think of human capital as a pyramid: a broad base of basic literacy, a middle tier of vocational and technical skills, and a narrow top of elite researchers and innovators. India built a strong top. China built the entire pyramid — and sequenced it bottom-up.

"India built islands of excellence in a sea of mediocrity. China built an entire continent."

The ASER problem: India achieved near-universal enrollment — but not learning. Annual Status of Education Reports (ASER) consistently show that 50%+ of Class V students cannot read a Class II text. Enrollment is a necessary but insufficient condition. China, through centralized standards and political accountability mechanisms, ensured that enrollment translated to actual learning at far higher rates.

The missing middle: vocational training

Manufacturing doesn't need IIT engineers — it needs millions of semi-skilled workers. This is the layer China built systematically and India almost entirely neglected. It is arguably the single most important structural difference.

05

Industrial Policy &
The Manufacturing Question

Education creates human capital. But human capital needs an industrial ecosystem to deploy into. China built that ecosystem deliberately — SEZs, export policy, supply chain integration. India didn't. The question of why India became service-led (rather than manufacturing-led) is not just about education. It is about industrial policy.

"Manufacturing success requires basic education, industrial policy, and state-led coordination — not just one of the three. China did all three. India did one."

China's SEZ model

1979
First Special Economic Zones
Shenzhen, Zhuhai, Shantou, Xiamen. Land, labour, and tax advantages concentrated in specific geographies. Export-oriented manufacturing given preferential treatment. Shenzhen had a population of 30,000 in 1979; today it has 17 million and houses global electronics supply chains.
1984
14 coastal cities opened
Foreign investment actively courted. Joint ventures required technology transfer. China was simultaneously building market access and absorbing foreign expertise.
2001
WTO Accession
China's entry into the WTO at this moment — after two decades of SEZ experience, workforce training, and export infrastructure — was decisive. India's WTO integration was more gradual and without the same manufacturing base to leverage it.
2010s
Moving up the value chain
From low-cost assembly to high-tech manufacturing. Huawei, BYD, DJI, CATL emerge. China begins competing in sectors previously dominated only by USA, Japan, and Germany.

Why SEZs worked in China but struggle in India:

China's SEZs worked because of the full stack: cheap literate labour (mass education), political will to acquire land and relocate people, state-built infrastructure, and global supply chain relationships built over decades. India's SEZs, introduced in 2005, faced land acquisition disputes, regulatory complexity, and a workforce not trained for factory-floor roles at scale. The instrument was copied; the preconditions were not.

Vietnam's lesson (very relevant): Vietnam entered global manufacturing supply chains by offering what China could no longer offer cheaply — low costs, political stability, and a compliant, basic-literate workforce. India has the population but not the configured workforce. Vietnam optimized for factories; India optimized for software engineers. Both are valid choices, but India's choice has lower employment multipliers.

Why India became service-led — and whether that was a choice

India did not consciously decide to become a services economy. It became one because that is where its inherited advantages (English, IITs, mathematics) met global demand (Y2K, outsourcing wave). After 1991 liberalisation, services had lower barriers to entry than manufacturing in India's regulatory environment. The path of least resistance led to Bengaluru, not to a factory floor.

India's accidental strength
The IT sector boom
The Y2K crisis and subsequent outsourcing wave created demand that exactly matched India's supply of English-speaking, technically trained graduates. Without planning it, India became the world's back office. This is worth ~$250B in IT-BPO exports today. A genuine success — but with low employment multipliers relative to manufacturing.
The structural trap
Services don't absorb mass labour
India adds ~12 million people to its working-age population every year. IT services employs 5 million total after 30 years of growth. Manufacturing, by contrast, can absorb semi-skilled workers at scale — as China demonstrated by employing 150+ million factory workers by the 2000s. India's growth model is structurally unable to absorb its demographic dividend.
China's counterfactual lesson
Both is possible
China built mass manufacturing and elite universities (Projects 211 and 985). The dichotomy between "elite education" and "mass manufacturing" is false — it requires the right sequencing and institutional capacity to pursue both simultaneously. South Korea and Taiwan also demonstrate this: strong manufacturing and strong universities, built in parallel.

Infrastructure, Investment
& State Coordination

Even with the right education and industrial policy, manufacturing requires physical infrastructure — ports, roads, power, and logistics. China invested at a scale and speed that India has not matched. Investment rates, infrastructure quality, and the state's ability to coordinate land, labour, and capital are the final piece of the puzzle.

What China built (1980–2010)

Ports & Logistics

  • 7 of world's 10 busiest ports are Chinese
  • Container throughput 10× India's
  • Inland logistics networks linked SEZs to ports
  • Average container dwell time 1–2 days

Power & Energy

  • Total installed capacity: 3,348 GW
  • India: 453 GW (7× smaller)
  • Reliable 24/7 industrial power essential for manufacturing competitiveness

Land & SEZ Execution

  • Single-party state enables land acquisition at scale
  • Industrial parks built in 12–18 months
  • India: land acquisition legally contested, multi-year delays

Rail & Roads

  • High-speed rail: 42,000 km (world's largest)
  • National expressway: 177,000 km
  • India: 99 km HSR (Ahmedabad-Mumbai, in progress)

India's constraints

Land acquisition
The single biggest bottleneck
India's Land Acquisition Act requires consent from affected communities, environmental impact assessments, and compensation at market rates. Legitimate democratic safeguards — but they make building industrial corridors and SEZs far slower than China's model.
Labour laws
Complex regulation → small firms
India's labour laws historically encouraged firms to stay small (under 100 workers) to avoid compliance requirements. Result: 80%+ of workforce in informal economy, no large-scale factory employment. Firms prefer Vietnam (and increasingly Bangladesh) for scalable low-cost manufacturing.
Power reliability
24/7 industrial power only recently achieved
Reliable electricity is a prerequisite for competitive manufacturing. India's power infrastructure has improved dramatically but still has regional gaps. Manufacturing firms bear higher backup generator costs than Chinese counterparts.
PLI schemes (2020+)
India's belated industrial policy response
Production-Linked Incentive schemes across 14 sectors are India's most serious industrial policy in decades. Early results are mixed — some sectors (mobile phones, APIs) are working; others face approval delays. The direction is right; execution is still being tested.

The investment rate gap matters enormously: China invests ~50% of GDP; India ~30%. This difference — compounded over 40 years — explains physical infrastructure gaps more than any single policy decision. Manufacturing requires upfront capital (factories, logistics, power). Countries that invest more simply build more. India's investment rate needs to rise significantly and be directed toward the right sectors.

07

What India Missed —
and What It Can Still Do

This is not a story of India failing. India's IT sector, pharmaceutical industry, space programme, and democratic institutions are genuine achievements. But the honest accounting of what was missed — and what can still be built — is important. The demographic window is still open.

"India didn't fail because it built IITs. It failed to build the rest of the system around them."
Missed · 1960s–1990s
Mass primary education quality
Enrollment expanded but quality did not keep pace. No equivalent of China's political accountability mechanism for learning outcomes. ASER reports document the learning crisis 60 years later. The consequence: a workforce that enrolled in school but did not acquire factory-floor or technical skills.
Missed · 1980s–2010s
Vocational ecosystem at scale
India's ITIs exist but were never tied to industry demand the way China's vocational schools were to its factories. Only ~2% of upper secondary students in vocational tracks vs 25% in China. The missing middle: tens of millions of technicians, CNC operators, quality inspectors, and logistics coordinators that a manufacturing economy needs.
Missed · 1988–2016
A TORCH equivalent
No national programme to systematically build incubators, science parks, and lab-to-market pipelines until Startup India (2016). China's Torch programme (1988) gave it a 28-year head start in building the infrastructure for innovation commercialisation.
Persistent · Today
R&D at 0.65% of GDP
India's GERD has barely moved from 0.6–0.7% for 20+ years while China reached 2.58%. India's private sector funds only 37% of R&D (vs China's 76%). The government is simultaneously funder and user — breaking the industry-academia innovation loop that drives private R&D growth.
Persistent · Today
Brain drain without retention
~36% of top JEE rankers migrate abroad. 37% of US S&E doctorates go to foreign students (largely Chinese and Indian), most staying in the US. India trains talent for the world without building the domestic research environments, salaries, and infrastructure to retain them.
Structural · Deep
The services-manufacturing duality
India's growth model generates GDP but insufficient employment. 12 million enter the workforce annually; formal manufacturing absorbs a fraction. The informal economy (80%+ of workers) is a symptom of the missing manufacturing base. Reversing this requires industrial policy, infrastructure, and vocational training working together.

What India can still do — and where it has genuine strengths

Strength · Use it

English + Digital + Young demographics

The demographic dividend window is open

  • India's median age is 28; China's is 39 and rising. The manufacturing workforce advantage belongs to India now, not China.
  • English-language proficiency is a structural advantage in the global knowledge economy that China does not have and cannot easily replicate.
  • India's digital public infrastructure (UPI, Aadhaar, ONDC) shows that state-led platform-building at scale is possible in a democracy.
Strength · Build on it

Pharma, Space, IT

Proven sectors with scalable templates

  • India supplies 20% of global generic medicines — manufacturing at scale, with IP protection, quality standards, and global markets. A template for other sectors.
  • ISRO's cost efficiency (Chandrayaan at <$100M vs NASA equivalents at $1B+) shows what focused state institutions can achieve.
  • These sectors demonstrate that India can build world-class manufacturing when the policy, talent, and regulatory environment align.
Priority · Most urgent

NEP 2020 + Vocational scaling

Fix the middle of the pyramid

  • National Education Policy 2020 emphasizes vocational integration from Class 6 onward. Implementation is the test — not policy design.
  • Target: 25–30% of upper secondary students in vocational tracks (from 2% today). Requires industry partnerships, not just government ITIs.
  • The Germany Dual System model — apprenticeships co-funded and co-designed by firms — is the benchmark.
Priority · Systemic

R&D at 2% of GDP

The private sector must lead

  • India's long-term goal of 2% GERD requires private sector R&D to grow from 37% to 60%+ of total. This requires tax incentives, procurement policies, and deep-tech park infrastructure.
  • PLI schemes are a start — but linking them to R&D investment requirements (as South Korea's chaebol model did) would compound the effect.
  • More IISERs, more research-intensive universities, more post-doctoral opportunities with competitive salaries to retain PhDs in India.

The honest synthesis: India's problem is not that it built IITs. It is that it didn't build the vocational layer, the incubation infrastructure, the industrial policy, and the primary education quality simultaneously. The IITs were a good strategic bet that paid off in IT and services. The failure was the missing middle — tens of millions of technicians, operators, and skilled workers that a manufacturing economy requires. That pipeline can still be built. The demographic window to do it closes by roughly 2040.
