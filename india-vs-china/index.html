The Development Divergence

India and China started at near-identical economic positions. Today China's GDP per capita is 5× higher. What happened — and what can India still do? Based on Prof. V.S. Ramamurthy's lecture.

By Ashutosh Nayak April 2026 Reading Notes & Data
The Core Thesis

From parity to a 5× gap in fifty years

In 1947–49, India and China reset together — same colonial shadows, same broken economies, near-identical GDP per capita. By 2024, China is the world's largest economy by PPP. India is third. The gap between them in per-capita terms is roughly 2.5×. This divergence was not inevitable.

The Core Thesis: China made a series of deliberate, sequenced interventions — Programme 863, TORCH, Project 211, Project 985, Double First Class — building institutions, R&D infrastructure, and innovation ecosystems across four decades. India, despite producing world-class engineers and scientists, underinvested in the system around talent: incubators, research funding, elite institutions, and science-industry linkages.

This is not a failure of talent. India has produced Nobel laureates, Turing Award winners, and the CEOs of most of the world's largest tech companies. It is a failure of institution-building — of creating the scaffolding that turns individual brilliance into collective capability.

Chapter 02 — China's Playbook

Four critical interventions that changed everything

Prof. Ramamurthy highlighted four landmark programmes. Each one targeted a different link in the innovation chain — from basic R&D, to commercialization, to elite universities, to world-class institutions. India had no comparable systematic response.

Programme 863 · March 1986
State High-Tech R&D Plan
Four scientists — Wang Daheng, Wang Ganchang, Yang Jiachi, Chen Fangyun — wrote to Deng Xiaoping warning that China was falling behind the global tech revolution. Deng approved it within 48 hours. The programme targeted 7 fields: biology, aerospace, IT, lasers, automation, energy, new materials. Initial government fund: 10 billion RMB (5% of all government spending in 1986). Inspired by Reagan's Strategic Defense Initiative.
120,000+ research papers, 8,000+ patents, 1,800+ national industry standards over 20 years. Provided the R&D backbone for China's later tech industries. Merged into National Key R&D Program in 2016.
Programme TORCH · August 1988
High-Tech Industrialization & Incubation
Approved by State Council to bridge the gap between lab and market. Created Science and Technology Industrial Parks (STIPs), Technology Business Incubators (TBIs), the Innofund seed-funding scheme, and a Venture Guiding Fund. The first incubator was built in Wuhan in 1987; Shanghai followed in 1988. The programme artificially created clustering — concentrating resources, talent, and capital to a critical threshold. Israel, Singapore and China are cited as the only three nations to successfully create innovation clusters by government design.
China now has 11,000–11,800 incubators & accelerators. India has ~950 (2023). China's incubators supported 620,000+ startups in 2018 alone. Created the infrastructure for the Alibaba, Baidu, Tencent era.
Project 211 · 1995
Strengthen 100 Key Universities
Named for the 21st century and 100 institutions. Goal: "strengthen approximately 100 institutions of higher education and key disciplinary areas as a national priority." Made research universities compete for major government grants, forcing them to build faculty, labs, and infrastructure. Every top Chinese research university of today was shaped by this programme. 112 universities were ultimately included.
Completely transformed Chinese higher education quality. Created the pipeline of researchers that produced China's publication explosion in the 2000s–2010s. India had no comparable programme — its IITs received good funding but the ecosystem around them was thin.
Project 985 · 1998 → Double First Class · 2015
World-Class Universities
Jiang Zemin declared China must have "a number of first-rate universities of international advanced level." 9 universities initially (C9 League = China's Ivy League), expanding to 39. Then Double First Class (2015/2017): 140 universities targeted to become world-class by 2050. Billions poured into faculty, labs, international hiring, and research infrastructure.
Peking & Tsinghua now rank in global top 15–25. China has 244/1,000 in ARWU 2025 (vs US: 200). China overtook USA in total Nature Index natural-sciences output in 2022.
What India did instead: India built excellent but scarce institutions (7 IITs by 2000 vs 112 Chinese universities under Project 211). India had no TORCH equivalent — the startup/incubation infrastructure was largely absent until Startup India (2016). India's GERD stayed at 0.6–0.7% of GDP; China's crossed 2.6% by 2023. The key asymmetry was not talent — India produced abundant talent — but the ecosystem to retain and leverage that talent domestically.

China's full strategic timeline

1956
12-Year Science & Technology Plan — China's first strategic S&T plan, identifying 57 key tasks including atomic energy, electronics, jet propulsion.
1978
Deng Xiaoping's "Four Modernizations" — Science & technology made one of four pillars of national renewal alongside industry, agriculture, and defense. Gaokao restored; universities reopened.
1986
Programme 863 — State High-Tech R&D, 7 strategic fields.
1988
TORCH Programme — Incubation, commercialization, STIPs. The most consequential programme India never replicated.
1991
Programme 973 — Basic research strengthening (complementing 863's applied focus). Later merged into National Key R&D Program.
1995
Project 211 — 112 elite universities in reform pipeline.
1998
Project 985 — World-class universities, C9 League. Peking and Tsinghua became global institutions.
2001
WTO Accession — Forced quality upgrades; China integrated into global supply chains at scale. India's WTO integration was more gradual.
2015–17
Double First Class + Made in China 2025 — 140 world-class universities; industrial upgrading in 10 key sectors (AI, EVs, semiconductors, biotech, aerospace).
2024
R&D spend reaches $496B — 2.6% of GDP. India's: ~$23B (~0.65% of GDP). China's business sector funds 76% of R&D; India's: only 37%.

India's bottleneck is not bachelor's degrees, it's PhDs and Masters. China produces 60,000 engineering PhDs per year; India produces ~17,000 (with funding disruptions during COVID further reducing this). The USA's 41,000 PhDs include 37% international students — majority Chinese and Indian — effectively brain-draining both countries. India needs to produce ~3× more PhDs and retain them with competitive salaries and research facilities.

The incubator gap is staggering: China has 11,000+ technology incubators; India has ~950 — an 11:1 ratio. China's incubators supported 620,000+ startups in 2018 alone. Chapter 07 — Institutions

Building the minds of nations

This is where Prof. Ramamurthy's lecture converged — India underbuilt research and teaching institutions during the crucial 1980–2010 period when China was building 100+ world-class universities under Projects 211 and 985.

Cumulative Elite Institutions Growth — India vs China (1947–2024)
India: IITs + IIMs + AIIMS combined. China: 985-project + 211 project universities combined milestone timeline. Note: China's 211/985 universities were existing institutions upgraded/funded, not built from scratch.
The key pattern: India added elite institutions slowly — averaging roughly one new IIT, IIM, or AIIMS per 3–4 years from 1947 to 2000. China, after Project 211 in 1995, rapidly brought 112 institutions into a reform and funding pipeline. The pace difference was decisive for research output in the 2000s–2020s.
Chapter 08 — Lessons & Path Forward

What India missed — and what it can still do

Prof. Ramamurthy's core argument: India didn't build the institutions and ecosystems while China was building them (1980–2010). But India's demographic window is still open. What are the specific gaps — and what can India do about them?

No "TORCH" Programme
India had no equivalent of China's 1988 Torch Programme — no systematic national programme to create technology parks, incubators, and commercialization pathways for decades. Startup India (2016) came 28 years later.
China: 11,800 incubators. India: 950 incubators in 2023.
Too Few Elite Universities
China funded 112 universities under Project 211 (1995) and 39 under 985 (1998). India's IITs only reached 16 by 2012 — 17 years after China's Project 211. The pipeline of researchers was simply too thin.
India: 7 IITs in 2000. China: 112 institutions in reform pipeline by 1995.
R&D at 0.65% of GDP
India's GERD has barely budged from 0.6–0.7% for 20+ years. China went from 0.9% (2000) to 2.58% (2023). Critically, India's private sector funds only 37% of R&D; China's: 76%. Government is both funder AND user in India — breaking the industry-academia loop.
India R&D 2024: ~$23B. China: $496B. USA: $950B+.
Late Liberalization
China opened its economy in 1978 (Deng's reforms) and joined WTO in 2001. India liberalized only in 1991, and remained more inward-looking for longer. China's export-led growth funded the R&D investment cycle.
In 1980, India's GDP (PPP) was larger than China's. By 2024, China is 5× bigger.
Brain Drain Unaddressed
IIT graduates emigrate at high rates. 25,000 IIT graduates have settled in the US since 1953. 37% of US S&E doctorates go to foreign students (largely Chinese and Indian), most staying in the US. India trains talent for the world without building systems to retain it.
India's researchers/million: 253. USA: 4,412. S. Korea: 8,714.
Weak Industry-Academia Links
India's incubators remain tied to government academic institutions where private capital remains limited. China built Productivity Promotion Centers (1,000+) specifically to link research and business.
India's business R&D share: 37%. China: 76%. Japan: 79%. S. Korea: 80%.
No Scale in PhD Pipeline
China: 60,000 engineering PhDs/year. India: 17,000 total S&E PhDs/year. The "raw material" for research-driven innovation — doctoral graduates — is critically short. IISER, NISER, and new IITs help, but the scale is still far behind.
India needs ~3× more PhD output to compete at China's level.
Deep-Tech vs Services Trap
India's startup success is real but concentrated in consumer apps and IT services. China invested $12.3B in AI and semiconductor startups in 2024 alone. India lacks fabrication plants, testing centers, and deep-tech labs at scale.
China deep-tech: BYD, DJI, CATL, Huawei. India: Zomato, Paytm, BYJU's.
What India must do — Prof. Ramamurthy's prescription
  1. Raise GERD to at least 2% of GDP by 2035 — requires private sector involvement (currently only 37% vs China's 76%)
  2. Build 5,000+ quality incubators with private capital — not just government-academic institutions
  3. Scale PhD output to 50,000+/year with competitive fellowships and world-class labs
  4. Create "Innovation Zones" equivalent to China's STIPs — deliberately clustering talent, capital, and infrastructure
  5. Deploy returnee programs competitive enough to attract diaspora back (like China's Thousand Talents Programme)
  6. Build more IISERs, research universities, and technology institutes — especially in underserved states
  7. Strengthen the IISc + IIT research ecosystem with sustained long-term funding, not project-to-project grants

India's strengths that China lacks (and can build on)

Democratic Institutions
Rule of law, independent judiciary, IP protection — China's IPR enforcement is improving but India's has been stronger historically. This matters for attracting global R&D centers.
English Proficiency
Natural advantage in global knowledge economy. India's engineers and researchers publish in English, collaborate internationally, and access global knowledge networks without translation barriers.
Young Demographics
India's median age is 28; China's is 39 and rising fast due to the one-child policy aftermath. India's demographic window for a workforce dividend is still wide open.
Software & Services Base
India's $250B+ IT services industry provides a platform to pivot toward product, AI, and deep-tech. Infosys, TCS, Wipro, and the startup ecosystem create talent density that can be redirected.
Space & Defense
ISRO's cost-efficient model (Chandrayaan at <$100M vs NASA equivalents at $1B+) shows India can punch above its weight in high-tech with the right institutional design.
Pharma & Biotech
India is the pharmacy of the world — 20% of global generic medicine exports. This sector shows what Indian manufacturing can achieve with the right policies, scale, and IP environment.
